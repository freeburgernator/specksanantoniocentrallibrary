Designed for the Future

Purpose, Program, and Users

Libraries are a means for society to distribute information. Traditionally, this information has been through books, but the digital age has brought increased throughput of information in new forms. Highly aware of this, the commissioners of the San Antonio Central Library wanted to create a flexible space for the community to continue to learn as technology changed the ways people learned and interacted with information.1 Furthermore, the governing librarians wanted to create an dramatic and welcoming space to attract guests and keep them there.1

To this end, the San Antonio Central Library has created a number of learning spaces. These spaces revolve around a central six-story atrium to draw guests in, but maintain their orientation in the otherwise confusing building.1 Areas include:2

  • Stacks
  • Computer Labs available to the public
  • Texana / Genealogy database server housing and access computers
  • Low Vision Reading Room
  • Music Production Studio
  • Terraces for events
  • 3D printers, tablets, and training computers in learning labs
  • Meeting rooms

This invites many people to the library, including:2

  • Readers
  • Children
  • English learners
  • The Visually impaired
  • Budding musicians
  • Job seekers who apply using the public computers
  • Employers who post on the public small business posting boards
  • Elderly who are trying to learn how to use computers
  • Teenagers who are interested in music, technology, computers, or reading
  • Artists, who can display their art
  • People interested in their ancestry
  • Book Event Attendees

Responding to a Need

Library Sources

Going South: The New San Antonio Main Library

By Turner Drexel

The San Antonio Central Library (referred to in this source as the “New San Antonio Main Library”) was located on the site of an abandoned Sears, which constructed the parking lot still in use today, on an odd irregular pentagon. Legorreta’s design was chosen primarily because it complied to the vision of the library and the reality of the cite, while being novel, inviting, and modern. The location and design were not without critique however, some believing that the structure should go further downtown or respect the local architecture more.

Turner, Drexel. “Going South: The New San Antonio Main Library.”Cite, 1991, pp. 10–13.

Building Libraries for the 21st Century: The Shape of Information

By Craig Sapatos, edited by Terry Webb

The Central Library, named so during construction to distinguish it from the old “Main Library,” was commissioned in response to a recognized need to expand the services of the library. Librarians maintained a major influence on the design of the building throughout its construction and interior design, including raising an additional $10 million to buy new furniture for the building. Of chief and continual concern was lowering maintenance costs, completing the project on time, and creating a flexible space for additional renovations. The library was a huge success, gathering over 10,000 visitors and 600 new library cardholders in its first week, and increasing daily visitors from 800 to 3,000.

Zapatos, Craig. Building Libraries for the 21st Century: the Shape of Information. Edited by Terry Webb, McFarland & Company, Inc., 2004. Print.

And Successful Despite Critique

Magazine Article

Library In Texas: A Shade Too Much?

From the New York Times

The building was certainly the focus of controversy. Legorreta painted the structure red because he thought it was “joyful.” Colorful characters disagreed, naming the color “don’t-look-at-it-with-a-hangover-red.” and “bleeding heart liberal red.” These criticisms bay betray a more dangerous anti-Mexican sentiment concerned about the loss of Anglo-heritage in the white-minority Texas city. Legorreta himself settled upon the nomenclature “enchilada red.”

The color was chosen only after construction had already started, and celebrated the openning of the building with a “paint the town red” celebration. The paint itself is high maintenance, probably requiring a recoating every seven years.

Nevertheless, the building was a huge success, increasing book circulation by 95%, and receiving positive reviews from locals.

The New York Times. "Library In Texas: A Shade Too Much?" The New York Times 26 Nov. 1996, p. 1001024.

Typical of Legorreta

The Architect

Ricardo Legorreta was a prolific Mexican architect. Trained at the National University of Mexico, his signature style was a mix of Mexico’s indigenous and colonial sensibilities with modern simplicity.1 A dear friend of Luis Barragan, Legorreta employed the same simple and auter base of simple shapes to maximize the emotional connection of the structure, but then added bright color and dynamic lighting capacity back into the architecture.2

Legorreta was entirely an emotional designer, believing structured design would gut the structure of the necessary character.1 He would not draft until he had already arrived at the shape of the structure.1 Elements and colors would be added simply because he felt it was necessary.2 Details would be added by later drafters after the shape had already been established.2

Legorreta received the American Institute of Architects Gold Medal in 2000 and the Praemium Imperiale arts prize of Japan in 2011.3 He passed away on December 30, 2011 at the age of 80.3 His firm, Legorreta + Legorreta, is continued by his son Victor, who continues to expound on the styles of his father in modern settings.3

It Is Undeniably Incongruent

Google Earth

The San Antonio Central Library is located in an urban setting, but not too close to the other towers of downtown San Antonio and the river walk. The intersection is indeed awkward, and the parallelogram shape of the building fits well with the streets, aligning each side to some street.

As noticeable as other features, the building is simply different than its surrounding parks, hospitals, schools, parking lots, and other buildings, in terms of color and shape. In this sense, it attempts to impose its own vision on the area, rather than conform to the dominant style. Whether this is a good or bad thing is up to the beholder.

It is certainly an imposing structure. They even colored the sidewalks.

Derided by Some

Blog Post

The “Project for Public Spaces” hosts a database of case studies called the “Hall of Shame Archive,” which documents complaints against local public spaces. One of these was logged against the San Antonio Public Library.

The post, which can be read in full here, brings to light two implications:

  • The building is hostile to those who approach by bus or pedestrians, as there is a single main entrance.
  • The structures distinctive style separates it from the rest of San Antonio.

The authors points about logistics difficulties are well taken. The building is very much oriented toward its parking lot, and seems to disregard the long road along the north side. However, the buses likely have to stop in this inconvenient location because of the realities of the road.

The distinction of the building certainly does abruptly separate it from the rest of downtown, and perhaps purposefully so. The architecture is inherently Mexican, unlike the southwestern blend that is usual in the rest of San Antonio. Whether this is representative of changing times or disrespectful of location is an opinion probably driven by debates of identity.

Overall, I can understand the concerns of this author, but disagree with the severity. Cosmopolitan cities are a collection of odd-balls, not uniformities.

Project For Public Spaces. “SAN ANTONIO CENTRAL LIBRARY.” Project For Public Spaces, Project For Public Spaces, 7 Jan. 2002, www.pps.org/places/san-antonio-central-library.

But a Success to Many.

Summary

The San Antonio Central Library was a bold move by the city, which brought a visually eccentric building to the downtown image. The building gathered controversy for its outward appearance, but succeeded in almost every demanded capacity. It not only increased readership, but created new spaces for students of all ages to learn. The building has become an icon not just of books, but of learning and adaptation to changing times.

These are certainly important qualities. Libraries are in many places a dying public space., yet projects such as this one, and the much newer Austin Central Library defy the trend.

Perhaps the need for libraries is best summarized by Annie Spence, a Detroit Librarian, speaking to this American Life:

“I always say that it's the only place-- well, it's one of the last places you can go that you don't have to buy or believe in anything to come in. You can just come, and we'll help you, no matter what your question is. We'll try to figure it out with you.”
